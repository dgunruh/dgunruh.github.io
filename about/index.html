About the Author

I am a physics PhD student at the University of California, Davis, specializing in condensed matter theory. I work with Gergely Zimanyi on studying key issues in novel solar technologies. In particular, I work on:

  1. Boosting carrier mobility in nanoparticle solar cells. In a standard solar cell, light from the sun which hits the solar cell creates carriers (electrons and holes), which need to travel through the solar cell to be harvested at electrodes and produce electricity. However, in nanoparticle solar cells, rather than being formed from a continuous material, the solar cell is made of (approximately) spherical nanoparticles which are separated from each other by organic ligands. When the carriers travel through the solar cell, they need to hop from nanoparticle to nanoparticle, which makes it difficult for them to reach their electrodes (they thus have what is called “low mobility”). My work uses a kinetic Monte Carlo (KMC) framework to study methods of enhancing carrier mobility in these systems, including investigating the formation of band-like transport, and the role played by extended defects.
  2. Mitigating performance degradation in silicon heterojunction (HIT) solar cells. HIT solar cells have the highest efficiency of all non-concentrator silicon solar cells. However, they suffer from faster-than-normal degradation over time, as their open-circuit voltage () decays by 0.5%-1% a year. To determine the leading degradation processes in these heterojunctions, I use machine-learning driven atomistic simulation methods. This has included developing the first machine-learning driven Gaussian approximation potential for Si-H interactions, which actively learns from density functional theory (DFT) measurements, and has successfully achieved near-DFT accuracy in predicting the energies, forces, and virial stresses when making measurements across a variety of hydrogenated silicon structural phases.
